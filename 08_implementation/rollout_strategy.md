# Rollout Strategy

## Purpose
This strategy defines how the governance operating system should be introduced across domains, teams, and workflows in a staged and manageable way.

## Objective
The objective is to avoid governance launch failure caused by trying to operationalize too much, too quickly, without ownership, workflow fit, or adoption support.

## Rollout principles
- roll out by priority, not by artifact count
- start with high-impact assets and recurring pain points
- introduce governance through working processes, not only presentations
- align rollout to existing business rhythms where possible
- focus on repeatable use, not one-time documentation
- make decision rights and stewardship visible early

## Recommended rollout sequence

### Phase 1: Priority assets and controls
Start with:
- critical reports
- shared analytical outputs
- sensitive or widely used datasets
- assets with recurring quality, access, or definition problems

Key operating artifacts:
- owner and steward assignments
- issue intake log
- access decision log
- DQ thresholds and controls
- basic glossary and dictionary entries

### Phase 2: Shared workflow integration
Expand governance into:
- stewardship forum cadence
- access request and review workflow
- change impact assessment for material changes
- issue escalation and root cause review
- KPI reporting and maturity check-ins

### Phase 3: Broader domain adoption
Expand to:
- additional domains
- lower-risk shared assets
- wider metadata coverage
- broader training and role enablement
- standardized management reporting

## Rollout model options

| Model | When to use | Strength | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pilot-based | Early-stage governance build | Fast learning and proof of value | Limited early coverage |
| Domain-by-domain | Multiple distinct business areas | Clear accountability and sequencing | Can be slower |
| Asset-tier-based | Strong risk tiering exists | Aligns effort to business criticality | Requires good inventory clarity |
| Hybrid | Most practical in real organizations | Balances speed and control | Requires strong coordination |

## Recommended approach
A hybrid rollout is usually strongest:
- begin with high-risk or high-visibility assets
- stabilize the core workflows
- expand by domain once early operating discipline is proven

## Prerequisites for rollout
Before expanding, confirm:
- owner and steward roles are named
- issue and access workflows exist
- thresholds or decision rules exist for priority cases
- escalation logic is clear
- communication and training support is available

## Common rollout risks
- governance launched as theory, not workflow
- no accountable owners for high-priority assets
- artifact overload with little day-to-day use
- inconsistent manager support
- weak communication about why changes matter
- no measurement of whether rollout is working

## Risk mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Role ambiguity | Confirm owner and steward assignments before rollout |
| Poor adoption | Train against real workflows and real use cases |
| Too much complexity | Start with minimum viable governance artifacts |
| Weak credibility | Show early improvements in issue handling, access traceability, and quality controls |
| Fragmented implementation | Use one roadmap, one forum structure, and one KPI view |

## Rollout checkpoints
At the end of each rollout phase, review:
- what was implemented
- what was actually used
- what remains unclear
- what issues recurred
- which roles need more support
- whether the next phase is ready

## Governance rule
Rollout should be treated as controlled operating change, not just document release.
